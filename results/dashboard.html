Observation: Women earn >$50K at only 10.9% vs 30.6% for men — a raw gap of 19.7 percentage points. But raw disparity alone doesn't prove structural bias: it could reflect different occupations or education levels. The next two analyses test exactly that.

Key finding: The income gap did not shrink after matching — it remained at 20.1 points (vs 19.7 raw). Even when comparing men and women with virtually identical qualifications, job types, and working hours, the disparity persists with statistical certainty (p < 2.2×10⁻¹⁶). This rules out "women just have different jobs" as the primary explanation and points to a deeper structural pattern.

Finding: The model predicts men as high-income nearly 3× more often than women (21.8% vs 7.3%), directly reproducing the real-world disparity found in Steps 1 and 2. A classifier trained on biased historical data automatically inherits that bias — this is why fairness-aware machine learning matters.

Bias mitigation: Exponentiated Gradient with demographic parity constraint

ε = 0.01 · same Random Forest base estimator · Fairlearn ExponentiatedGradient · Python

Demographic parity difference 0.145 0.005 −96% ✓
Equalized odds difference 0.062 0.285 +360% ↑
Female selection rate 7.3% 16.0% +8.7 pts
Male selection rate 21.8% 16.5% −5.3 pts
Before mitigation After mitigation
DPD: 0.145 before, 0.005 after. EOD: 0.062 before, 0.285 after.
Fairness impossibility theorem — empirically confirmed: Mitigation achieved near-perfect demographic parity (0.145 → 0.005, a 96% reduction) at only a −1.4pt accuracy cost. However, equalized odds worsened fourfold (0.062 → 0.285). This illustrates the well-documented result (Kleinberg et al., 2016; Chouldechova, 2017) that when true outcome rates differ between groups, no classifier can simultaneously satisfy both demographic parity and equalized odds. The appropriate fairness criterion depends on the deployment context and which harms matter most.
